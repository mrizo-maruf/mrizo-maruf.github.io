title: "Real Problems and Fake Problems"
---
Fake vs Real problems in science. What is "real", what is "fake"?
---

There is a distinction that civilization rarely makes explicit, yet everything depends on it — the difference between a real problem and a fake one.

A fake problem is not necessarily trivial. It can be technically sophisticated, financially enormous, and absorb the lifetimes of brilliant people. What makes it fake is simpler and more damning: it exists to serve appetite, not to remove a genuine constraint on human life. The algorithm that keeps you scrolling thirty seconds longer. The virtual environment engineered for pleasure. The delivery service that brings comfort faster. These are not problems in any deep sense. They are appetites dressed in the language of innovation. Solving them does not expand what is possible for humanity. It deepens the groove of what we already do.

A real problem is different in kind, not just degree. It stands at a boundary — a place where human capability currently stops. People are dying because a collapsed building cannot be entered. A disease cannot be treated because no instrument is precise enough to reach it. An ocean floor remains unmapped because no system can navigate it autonomously. A planet sits forty light-seconds away, unreachable by real-time human guidance. These are not inconveniences. They are walls. And walls, unlike appetites, can be permanently removed.

History's greatest scientists understood this instinctively. The astronomers of the Islamic Golden Age were not gazing at stars out of idle curiosity. They faced urgent, concrete walls — how to determine the direction of Mecca from anywhere on Earth, how to fix a calendar that governed agriculture and religion, how to navigate trade routes across featureless deserts and open oceans. In dismantling those walls they built algebra, optics, and trigonometry as byproducts. The real problem, pursued seriously, generates knowledge that outlasts the problem itself by centuries.

This is the deepest argument against fake problems — not that they are immoral, but that they are *wasteful* in a cosmic sense. Every generation receives a fixed quantity of human genius. The question each generation must answer, usually without asking it explicitly, is what that genius will be aimed at. Aimed at appetite, it produces sophistication in the service of comfort. Aimed at real constraints, it produces new possibility — things that were simply not achievable before, permanently added to the human repertoire.

The young scientist faces this question with particular urgency, because the choice of problem is made early and compounds over time. A career aimed at fake problems can be successful by every conventional measure — funded, published, cited, rewarded. And yet at its end it will have left the walls standing. The constraints that matter will remain exactly where they were.

Real problems are harder to choose because they offer no guarantee. The boundary may not yield in one career, or two. The Islamic astronomer did not live to see the full civilization his mathematics would build. But this is precisely what makes the choice meaningful. To aim at a real wall, knowing you may only scratch it, is an act of genuine will — a contribution to something larger than a single life, a single paper, a single benchmark.

Nietzsche asked whether you could affirm your life so completely that you would choose to live it again, infinitely. It is a useful test for research directions too. The work aimed at keeping someone scrolling, at making the comfortable more comfortable — could you affirm that infinitely? Or does something in you already know that it is beneath what you are capable of?

The walls are real. The people on the other side of them are real. The only question is whether you will spend your genius on the walls, or on the appetite.
